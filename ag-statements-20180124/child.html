Scott Drury* D

State Representative

Increase AG investigations of police departments and indict bad cops

Drury promised to bring criminal charges against officers. Historically, the attorney general in Illinois has focused more on consumer protection than criminal prosecutions.

“You need to send the message that [misconduct is] not going to be tolerated,” Drury said. “I know that the people in Illinois have become comfortable with this notion that the attorney general can’t do criminal investigations, but I believe that is a very active question, and certainly one that I am going to look into and test when I am in office.”

*On Friday, Drury was removed from the ballot by a Cook County judge because of an objection related to his economic statement of interest. He is appealing the decision.

Sharon Fairley D

Former head of Chicago’s Civilian Office of Police Accountability

Create statewide use-of-force policy and provide assistance and training to cash-strapped police departments

Fairley said there needs to be a statewide agency responsible for reviewing police shootings that goes beyond the current system, which only involves a criminal investigation. For that to work, she said, the state needs uniform guidelines for when an officer can use lethal force. Fairley also said the attorney general’s office can “really help” by offering training and other assistance to smaller departments facing funding shortages.

“We need to use policy to raise the level so that we are authorizing officers to use force under circumstances that we as a community believe are appropriate,” she said.

Aaron Goldstein D

Former public defender

Sue cities and towns to establish community-driven, court-enforced reform plans

Goldstein said he would use Madigan’s lawsuit against the city of Chicago as a model for suing other problem departments, but with one big difference: more community involvement.

“The community needs to be involved and have enforcement power in these consent decrees,” he said.

Gary Grasso R

Former mayor of Burr Ridge

Offer assistance to under-resourced departments

Grasso said he would pressure local departments to do reviews, and he would offer help to the ones who can’t do it themselves.

“Not only would we use the bully pulpit, we would use the resources of the office to make sure these investigations are done,” he said.

Erika Harold R

Attorney

Push legislation to require independent reviews of policies and procedures after police shootings, and create civilian oversight agency for the suburbs

Harold said she is looking at two options: To advocate for the Illinois General Assembly to expand a 2015 law so police shootings are reviewed for things beyond criminality, and to create an agency similar to Chicago’s Civilian Office of Police Accountability to investigate police shootings in the suburbs. She said she is still “exploring which direction” would be accepted by relevant stakeholders, like suburban police chiefs and city leaders, and “bring about concrete changes most quickly and effectively.”

“The attorney general has to work in tandem with other stakeholders … and it’s important to not only propose laws that the general assembly will hopefully adopt, but also see if there’s another structure that can bring about change within the suburban police departments as well,” she said.

Renato Mariotti D

Former federal prosecutor

Require local departments to conduct reviews and issue public reports for police shootings

Mariotti said he would issue a directive that requires departments to review if officers followed policies and procedures, a similar tactic is used in New Jersey.

“If people don’t trust their police, they aren’t gonna go to them and be witnesses in cases. They aren’t gonna cooperate with the police. So it’s something that has a big impact for a limited cost,” he said.

Pat Quinn D

Former Illinois Governor

Work with state lawmakers to pass reform legislation

Quinn said state lawmakers need to pass legislation requiring reviews of police shootings to see if the officers followed department policies and procedures.

“It’s something the General Assembly has to address together with the attorney general and state’s attorneys from across the state, as well as police departments,” he said.

Kwame Raoul D

State senator

Push legislation to license officers and lawsuits against problem police departments

Raoul said he’d use the power of the office to advocate for a system that would license officers and allow for those licenses to be revoked for bad actions. Raoul said that would allow the state to make sure officers involved in bad shootings were stripped of their law enforcement powers. Raoul also said he would sue police departments that weren’t properly monitoring officers.

“[A method to revoke a license] exists for medical doctors, for pharmacists, for all sorts of professions — it exists for hairdressers and for barbers. But it does not exist for police officers,” he said.

Nancy Rotering D

Mayor of Highland Park

Push state laws requiring reviews of police shootings and get more resources to local departments

Rotering said she’d advocate for a state law requiring reviews of police shootings, but she said it would only work with a helping hand to smaller departments.

“We’ve got to first make sure those departments have the resources. Unfunded mandates are devastating,” she said. “A budget is the entity’s best explanation of its policy priorities. You cannot make these demands without putting the resources behind them.”

Jesse Ruiz D

Former member of the Chicago Board of Education and Illinois State Board of Education

Expand the scope of state police investigations, implement police licensing, create a statewide use-of-force policy, and provide training for officers

Ruiz said police departments should get a uniform amount of money, or other support like training, from the state. The system would work similar to the way school districts are funded. Ruiz also said he’d create a statewide use-of-force policy and push for police licensing. He added that since State Police are already looking at the criminality of police shootings, they should also review if the officers followed the policy and procedures laid out by their local departments.

“The state police go in and investigate for criminal wrongdoing but not for procedural wrongdoing, and it seems that the scope of the investigation should be expanded,” he said.
